Purigen: Polish Your Water Clarity

My go-to breakdown on Purigen: why it’s the polish I rely on for crystal clarity, where it lives in the sump, and the one hilarious drawback.

I used to stay away from anything that sounded like chemical filtration. No bottled bacteria, no fancy resins — just sponges, plants, and patience in the hunt for clear aquarium water. Then I tried Seachem Stability, and it nudged me to rethink things. I hacked old hang-on-back filters so I didn’t have to keep buying disposable cartridges — I’d cut big filter sponges to size, rinse them out, and reuse them for months. The downside was that whenever I swapped or deep-cleaned them, random bacterial blooms followed. Once I started using Stability, that issue basically disappeared. Sometimes you really do need more than a sponge or a pothos vine — sometimes a little helper earns its spot.

A while back, I watched a YouTube video by AQUAPROS titled “This is ACTUALLY The Method For Crazy Clear Aquarium Water...”. The creator didn’t drag it out — just a couple of minutes in, he said flat out, “It’s Purigen.” That stuck with me. Months later, I remembered it, wanted that same level of crystal clarity, and finally gave it a try.

My sump setup is simple: a 5-gallon chamber with layered sponges — coarse, medium, fine, and an ultra-fine polishing pad — followed by Purigen, then it goes to the freshwater refugium, where there are lava rocks, mosses, fast-growing floating plants, and finally the return pump. Purigen’s the polish — the final step in my mechanical filtration chain before the water moves on to the refugium and biological filtration stage.

I used to love that tea-tinted blackwater vibe. It looked earthy and calm, but then I decided I wanted to chase those deep red-colored plants. So I pivoted: clear water, strong light, and let the plants show off. About a month after adding Purigen, the difference in clarity was obvious. The surface shimmer reflected light in a way that felt alive — like wiping a soft film off the entire aquarium for consistently clear aquarium water. Purigen absorbs any leftover tannins.

Quick teach moment: Purigen isn’t carbon. It’s a synthetic adsorbent that traps dissolved organics before they break down into nitrates. Think of it as a water polish — not a fix, not a crutch. It doesn’t change pH or hardness. It just keeps things crisp between good maintenance.

There’s one funny downside, though: your water gets so clear that you’ll start noticing every speck of algae on the glass. Purigen doesn’t make algae — it just removes the haze that used to hide it. I actually ended up buying a magnetic in-tank algae scraper (mine was from Temu — that’s going to be its own write-up soon). So in a way, Purigen quietly bullies you into keeping your glass spotless. Honestly? That’s a win.

As for maintenance, you can recharge Purigen with a bleach-and-neutralizer process. I haven’t done it myself yet, so I won’t vouch for it, but a lot of hobbyists swear by two recharges and say they get close to a year out of one bag. Personally, I get about two to three months per bag without recharging in a heavily planted, fairly stocked tank with weekly water changes. You can — and it’s easier than you think — keep rotating fresh bags to hold that water polish while you learn the recharge routine.

Where Purigen shines for me:

  • After trims or rescapes when fine debris and tannins spike
  • On photography days when I want that book-quality look

Where it doesn’t shine: fixing poor maintenance. If your filter’s clogged or nitrates are spiking, Purigen won’t save the day — it’s the polish, not the paint, and clear aquarium water still depends on regular upkeep.

I’ve come to really trust Seachem’s lineup for the fundamentals — Stability, Prime, and now Purigen. No sponsorship here, just experience. I don’t use their fertilizers, but when it comes to a dependable water polish and startup stability, they’ve earned their reputation.

Bottom line: If you love the look of perfectly clear water where you can see every ripple and root, Purigen earns its space in your filter. Just be ready to enjoy that crystal clarity — and to spot everything, including the algae you didn’t know was there.
